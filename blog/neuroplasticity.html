Mindset - Neuroplasticity and Growth

Neuroplasticity

How does understanding the principles of neuroplasticity benefit people?

Understanding the idea of neuroplasticity, the idea that the brain is malleable, and that intelligence can be acquired, not just have been born with, empowers learners to apply themselves. This realisation can help unleash people’s true potential without being held back by their perceived intelligence limitations.

It also provides some ideas as to some of the best ways to study; namely, repeated practice and learning in the context of existing knowledge. Reminding learners this is a powerful aid in their motivation and morale.

How might you engage with the principles of neuroplasticity for your own benefit?

Firstly, reminding myself of the malleable brain concept by reviewing my blog post and the notes on the student portal will be helpful to me as self-doubt can often come about along with feelings of not being smart enough.

Moreover, I want to practice coding as well as engaging with the core material we are learning often – continuing to put these to practice and engage with the course content to get a better grasp of it and strengthen ‘pathways’ in my brain.

I will also try to relate difficult concepts to things I am already familiar with to try learn in context.

"Practice makes permanent. The more times the network is stimulated, the stronger and more efficient it becomes.” - Judy Willis

What are some of the ways to increase your neuroplasticity?

  • Physical activity strengthens the brain. Exercise — aerobic exercise in particular — can lead to improvements in cognitive abilities like learning and memory. Exercise also helps improve fine motor coordination and brain connectivity, and may protect against cognitive decline.

  • Create art – such as drawing and painting can enhance creativity and improve cognitive abilities. They can also help create new pathways and strengthen existing ones.

  • Practicing a new language – research shows that learning a new language can boost grey matter density in the brain. Grey matter houses areas associated with attention, language, memory, emotions and motor skills.

What resource did you find particularly useful or engaging?

I found Healthline's 6 Ways to Rewire Your Brain to be interesting as learning about the ways in which I can improve my neuroplasticity makes me want to make time for activities I enjoy, but struggle to prioritise. I want to get back to practicing and learning more Japanese, exercising, and making art and music.

Growth Mindset

What it is and why it is relevant?

The growth mindset is the belief that your abilities are not fixed but can change.

"Science has also revealed that our beliefs about ourselves, what we think and feel about our abilities, and our beliefs about success and failure hugely influence our outcomes."

Having a growth mindset involves:

  • Embracing challenges
  • Persisting in obstacles
  • Seeing effort as necessary
  • Learning from feedback
  • Being inspired by others
  • Celebrating not knowing - as an opportunity to grow

Did anything surprise you and change for you in this exploration?

The studies used as examples and proof surprised me as it showed how much of an impact a growth mindset can have on outcomes.

I have struggled a lot with believing in myself and abilities in the past, so I want to continue to remind myself of this.

How will you integrate growth mindset into your learning journey?

Understanding that the brain is malleable and capable of tremendous change allows people to exert themselves in a way that allows them to grow and learn. Knowing that your abilities and skills are not fixed, and that you don’t have to have natural talent in order to be good at something, gives people enough hope to be able to believe in themselves and change.

Conquering small tasks, and succeeding repeatedly can help grow this confidence.
